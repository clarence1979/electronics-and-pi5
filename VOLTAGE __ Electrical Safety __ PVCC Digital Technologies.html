// MODULE 01

What is electricity, really?

Electricity is the movement of tiny charged particles (electrons) through a material. To understand the danger, you only need three quantities — and a tap.

💧 The water-pipe model

Imagine water in a pipe. Voltage is the water pressure pushing it along. Current is how much water actually flows past you each second. Resistance is how narrow the pipe is — it fights the flow.

⚡ Voltage (push)Volts (V)
🌊 Current (flow)Amps (A)
🧱 Resistance (squeeze)Ohms (Ω)
Ohm's LawI = V ÷ R

It's invisible

You can't see, smell or hear mains electricity. A live wire looks identical to a dead one. Never assume — always switch off and check.

It's instant

Electricity travels at near the speed of light. There is no "react in time." Your safety depends entirely on prevention, not reflexes.

It always wants out

Electricity desperately seeks the easiest path back to earth. If that path runs through you, you become part of the circuit.

// MODULE 06

When it goes wrong

If you find someone being shocked, your instinct is to grab them — don't. You'll become the next casualty. Follow this order instead.

Don't touch them

If they're still in contact with the source, touching them puts the same current through you. Keep your hands off.

Cut the power

Switch off at the wall and the main switchboard, or pull the plug by the lead. Removing the source is the single most important action.

Call 000

Ask for ambulance. Stay on the line. In Australia, dial Triple Zero. Use a phone, not the person, to call for help.

If safe — use non-conductive help

If you can't cut power, push them clear with something dry and non-conductive like a wooden broom handle. Never use anything metal or damp.

DRSABCD & CPR

Once the source is off and it's safe, check response and breathing. Begin CPR if trained. Electric shock can stop the heart, and CPR buys time.

// MODULE 07 · REBUILD LAB

Safety in our hardware lab

In the ReBuild Lab you'll open up real PCs. Most components run on safe low voltage — but two things will still hurt you, and one thing you'll hurt without even feeling it.

MAINS · LETHAL

The PSU is off-limits

Never open a power supply unit. Its capacitors can hold a lethal 300V+ charge for minutes after it's unplugged. Faulty PSU? Bin it, don't crack it.

HEAT · BURNS

Power down & unplug first

Always shut down and pull the cable before working inside a case. Hold the power button 5s to discharge residual energy. Let hot components cool.

ESD · SILENT

Static kills chips

You can't feel a 200V static zap, but it fries RAM and CPUs instantly. Wear the anti-static wrist strap and touch bare metal before handling parts.

⚙️ Why low voltage ≠ no risk

Inside the case you mostly meet 3.3V, 5V and 12V — too low to push dangerous current through dry skin. The danger flips the moment mains 230V enters: the wall lead, the PSU, the monitor's internal board. Treat anything that plugs into the wall as live and lethal until it's unplugged and proven dead. Low voltage earns your confidence; mains voltage earns your fear.

Q1What actually causes injury or death in an electric shock?
Voltage pushes, but it's the CURRENT through your body that stops the heart. Even ~50 mA can be fatal.
Q2Australian mains power runs at approximately…
Australia uses 230V / 50Hz — more than enough to kill, which is why every safety rule matters.
Q3Which path of current through the body is the MOST dangerous?
Hand-to-hand sends current straight across the heart — the deadliest route.
Q4Why is water near electricity so dangerous?
Wet skin can drop your resistance from 100kΩ to ~1kΩ, multiplying the current that flows through you.
Q5What does an RCD (safety switch) do?
An RCD detects tiny leakage current (~30mA) and trips in under 30ms — fast enough to save a life.
Q6You see someone being shocked and still touching the source. First action?
Never touch them while live — you'll be shocked too. Cutting the power is the priority.
Q7In the hardware lab, which component must you NEVER open?
PSU capacitors store a lethal charge for minutes after unplugging. Never open one.
Q8Why wear an anti-static wrist strap when handling PC components?
A static zap you can't feel (even 200V) instantly destroys RAM and CPUs. The strap safely grounds you.
